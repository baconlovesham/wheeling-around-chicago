Background

Chicago is expericing a cycling surge

There is a greater adoption of cycling as a form of transport

Since the 1980s, the number of cyclist commuters has grown by 12x (Active Transportation Alliance 2020)

Bike sharing culture is only expected to grow

Divvy hit a record high of more than 5.6 million bike trips in 2022, over 60% higher than 2019 (City of Chicago 2023)

Chicago city is committed to a cycling strategy

The city is committing over $17m to expand Chicago’s bike network. Divvy is adding 3,000 new bikes and 250 stations by 2025 (City of Chicago 2023).

There is rising interest in cycling/ bike sharing as a form of urban transport

This is fuelled in three areas:

Limitations of automobile-dependent transport planning

Cities that are dependent on automobile as a form of urban transport often are faced with parking problems, air pollution, reduced amenities, livability and climate change issues (Hickman & Banister 2014). Cycling on the other hand, enables urban mobility without producing these negative externalities.

Rapid growth in bike sharing services mainstreaming the use of cycling as urban transport

Today, more than 850 cities worldwide have bike sharing programs. From a consumer perspective, the availability, affordability and convenience of service supported by technological advancements have made bike sharing a viable, if not, economical option.

Big data on cycling patterns now available

Previously, data on cycling patterns was deficient as data on cyclists and their trips were not readily collected. However with GPS technologies, live point data and journey data now collected by bike sharing companies, the data gap is now filled.

Yet, despite cycling's benefits, studies have also raised questions about the equitability of cycling as a form of transport

Benefits Studies on equitability of cycling
Improved physical health (Clockston and Rojas-Rueda 2021) Variation in demographics of who’s cycling at a global level (Tortosa et al. 2022).
Reduced energy consumption and carbon footprint (Saltykova et al. 2022) People from deprived areas are at higher risk of slight and serious cycling injuries in England (Tortosa et al. 2021).
Improving urban mobility in the last mile (Midgey 2009) Wealthier populations get greater access to bike sharing stations (Anaya-Boig et al. 2022).

Our motiviations for this web GIS project are three fold

  1. There is a booming cycling scene in Chicago with room for improvement with some describbing it as bike friendly but not bike safe. How might we improve this?
  2. How can we optimise for Chicago's growing bicycle scene? All ciites are unique and not all cities have succeeded at implementing cycling as form of transport. How can we make it count now?
  3. Can we provide an interactive map to raise awareness on the insights? This one is for you - the urban planners, policymakers, the everyday cyclist, member of Chicago city.
